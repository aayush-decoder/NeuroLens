Chapter 1: Friction and Comprehension

Digital reading tools often include many controls that compete for attention. Students can lose context when panels, popups, and notifications interrupt reading flow.

A focused interface keeps only the text and removes visual noise while scrolling. This design supports comprehension by reducing task switching and preserving working memory.

Telemetry can estimate friction implicitly. If a learner pauses too long on a paragraph, scrolls back repeatedly, or slows down sharply, the system can infer comprehension difficulty.

Adaptive text support can simplify selected phrases, add short inline definitions, and provide contextual hints. These changes should be subtle and reversible.

At the end of reading, the system summarizes high-friction concepts and produces a compact review sheet. Learners can revisit difficult ideas quickly before moving forward.

For multilingual support, difficult English words can be mapped to familiar cognates in Hindi, Spanish, French, or Telugu.

Long sessions may increase fatigue. Ambient eye-strain adjustment can gradually increase line spacing, soften contrast, and rebalance typography for comfort.
